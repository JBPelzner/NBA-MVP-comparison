What's so great about an MVP?

Basics and an overview of NBA MVPs

Every year, a panel of sports journalists vote on the National Basketball Association Most Valuable Player Award. The player chosen each year is regarded as the best player in the most talented professional basketball league in the world. The MVP usually averages outstanding numbers in basic statistical categories like Points, Rebounds, and Assists while leading their team to more victories than most or all of the other 29 NBA teams. This chart allows us to compare some statistical categories that encompass the MVP's individual and team performance. Notice the large differences between the "Average NBA Player" and our MVPs.

Who makes their team better? Pt. I

Offensive and defensive ratings

Let's take a look at how our MVPs play on both sides of the ball. Here we're looking at Offensive Rating and Defensive Rating (ORTG and DRTG). A team's ORTG is how many points it scores per 100 possessions. The more, the better! Its DRTG is how many points it allows opponents to score per 100 possessions. Fewer is better here. Likewise, a player's ORTG and DRTG are how many points they score or have scored against them per 100 possessions. A player with a higher ORTG is better at scoring, and a player with a lower DRTG is better at defending. [nba.com]

Each team's ORTG and DRTG reflects the team's overall talent and includes the contribution of its MVP. However, we can observe the disparity in ability between a team and its MVP, with some MVPs being much better than their team at offense and/or defense. Players with the largest positive difference from their team improve their team the most.

Who makes their team better? Pt. II

Net ratings

When Offensive and Defensive Rating are combined, we get Net Rating (NetRTG). This is the difference in points per 100 possessions scored between a team and all its opponents on average. Similarly, NetRTG for an individual is how many more points the player scores than they allow per 100 possessions.

In recent years, non-overtime games have averaged about 100 possessions. Therefore, a team with a NetRTG of 6.9 for a season beat their opponent on average by about 6.9 points throughout that season. [nba.com]

MVP Stats: A More Through Examination

Of the displayed statistics, Box Plus/Minus and True Shooting Percentage are more complex metrics that warrant further investigation. As you can see from the chart, Stephen Curry's 2015-2016 season illustrates that he had the highest values of any of the respective MVP's for these statistics. The subsequent sections of this report will begin to examine those statistics more thoroughly, to explore the factors that contribute to Curry's excellence in these fields.

Box Plus/Minus (BPM)

Box Plus/Minus (BPM), while being a very complex derived statistic, is defined as a box score estimate of the points per 100 possessions a player contributed above a league-average player, translated to an average team. Put simply, it estimates a basketball playerâ€™s contribution to the team when that player is on the court.

Being that Box Plus/Minus is an aggregate of Offensive BPM and Defensive BPM, it is necessary to plot all three of these statistics independently to evaluate the outliers in each category and to see learn how a player's total BPM value is broken down.

True Shooting Percentage (TS%)

True Shooting Percentage (TS%) is defined as a shooting percentage that factors in the value of 3-Point Field Goals and Free Throws in addition to conventional two-point field goals. [nba.com]

Thus, it is relevant to analyze the distribution of True Shooting Percentages for players relative to their 3-Point accuracy and Free Throw accuracy, while allowing filtering for respective numbers of these shots taken (3PA and FTA). Additionally, the distributions may vary based on overall productivity in terms of Points Per Game (PPG).

Effective Field Goal Percentage (eFG%)

Effective Field Goal Percentage (eFG%) is defined as a measure of field goal percentage that adjusts for the fact that made 3-point goals are 1.5 more valuable than made 2-point field goals. [nba.com]

Similar to True Shooting Percentage, it is relevant to analyze the distribution of Effective Field Goal Percentages for players relative to their 3-Point accuracy, while filtering for number of 3-Pointers Attempted (3PA) and overall productivity in terms of Points Per Game (PPG). This can provide insight on the most impactful shooters, with a statistic that is more refined than True Shooting Percentage (TS%).

Conclusion

Stephen Curry's 2015-2016 Season was One-Of-a-Kind

In summary, when we compare all of the NBA's MVPs from the 2012-2013 season through the 2017-2018 season, Stephen Curry's 2015-2016 season stands above the rest. Curry's contibutions to his team were bar none, as evidenced by his superior Net Rating among the MVPs, as well as by his superior Net Rating differential with respect to the other MVPs. Furthermore, his exceptional Box/Plus Minus score, highlighted by the offensive component of that statistic, reiterates the incedible impact that Curry had on his team during that season. Investigation of his True Shooting Percentage and Effective Field Goal Percentage relative to other MVPs and the rest of the league help to explain how he was able to make such an impact. For players of his usage and productivity, his 3-Point Field Goal Percentage and Free Throw Percentage were unmatched, and his accuracy over high volume in these respects truly underscores the unmatched quality of his performance during this season. Stephen Curry's 2015-2016 season, in which he lead the Warriors to a record-setting 73 wins, makes him the greatest MVP of the modern era.
